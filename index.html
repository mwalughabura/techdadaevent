About

Sub-Saharan Africa is poised for major population growth, and by mid-century, 40% of the world’s children will live on the continent.  It is unlikely that that the current education model will be sufficient for preparing the incoming generations for life in a 21st century world, especially unless current challenges are overcome.   Even as Kenya sets itself up to be a regional tech hub, Information Technology (IT) education in Turkana lags far behind where it needs to be.  A lack of qualified teachers, inadequate infrastructure, outdated curricula, and prohibitively high Internet costs are all barriers that prevent Kenyan youth from gaining skills relevant for the digital world.

The challenges are even more substantial for young women and girls, who are more likely than boys to be out of school. In Turkana region, computers are still considered by many to be a male domain, and many young women shy away from computer spaces such as cyber cafes due to fear of harassment.    TechDada is an initiative that seeks to equip girls and young women with mindsets and skills that will enable them to confidently pursue 21st-century opportunities. Its  vision is to ensure more young women in East Africa pursue careers in technology, design, and business with a focus on solving real problems in local communities and globally.   Tech Dada has created a curriculum and courses that increase awareness of tech opportunities for women and try to overturn the gender stereotypes that prevent young women from entering the field of technology.

Techdada is now active in two places: Nairobi, Kibera and Turkana, Lodwar. In Kibera we are mostly concentrating on empowering girls in high schools. In Turkana, our focus is on empowering the ladies in a tech program called learning Lions. We have trained so far about 30 ladies in the last three cohorts. We train on excellence, personality, character, vision and goals, self worth and confidence, planning, being professional, hope for young mothers, sexual reproductive health, stewardship, servant leadership. These sessions are engaging and practical. We encourage the girls to be more intentional in their pursuit irregardless of the title. Beneficiaries of the program report encouraging results from even a few short sessions.  The girls are more confident speaking in front of a group, more articulate in sharing their ideas, more interested in technology and more aware of the challenges facing women in the industry, as well as the tools they can use to overcome those challenges. We have also conducted workshops with more than 200 girls in the region on the myths, challenges and benefits of technology. We have visited a girl’s school as well where we had an interesting day engaging upper primary school girls on the awesomeness of technology and the diverse careers they can land themselves in with tech knowledge.
